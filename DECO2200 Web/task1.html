Mental Health and Gambling

Key Findings in this Section

Pathological Gambling was recognised as a clinical disorder in 1980 and was introduced into the DSM (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders) (NSW Parliament, 2014) This enabled a scaffold for classifying and identifying problem gambling by using diagnostic criteria. This criteria is recognised in court and it provided a common-use language for clinicians to communicate and understand problem gambling. This also opened the field to research and to understanding how problem gambling occurs and how to treat it.

A human's intrinsic motivation to gamble is based within the field of prospect. This is what promotes problem gambling. This prospect falls under the heading of excitement generation over the idea of being able to win large prizes (usually of some monetary value). For problem gamblers, the "motivational strength" for this desire to gamble is difficult to control. This is due to their constant need to uphold this level of arousal and motivation to succeed in winning this prospect of a prize. (APS Review Paper, 2010). This is clearly evident in theories such as 'chasing a loss' - this is where a gambler takes larger risks (usually with bigger or more bets) in hopes to undo a streak of losses. (NSW Parliament, 2014). This depicts a clear motivation weakness where they simply cannot walk away from the machine.

Major Theoretical Approaches to Problem Gambling

Key Findings in this Section

Learning/ Learnt Behaviour Theory

By the use of positive and negative reinforcement, the illicit and innate human response of an emotional response can condition a person to associate a certain action with a certain feeling. (APS Review Paper, 2010) (Dickerson, M. 1993)

    Gambling formulates this behaviour by conditioning winning and losing to form specific emotional reactions by a person.
    Winning, in gambling, is repeatably associated with positive gambling-related environmental cues, such as the lights and sounds by the machine, and the winning of a prize usually of monetary value.
    With this conditioning, it results in an exposure of arousal and excitement (APS Review Paper, 2010)
    By this conditioning, a person then associates the feeling of excitement and arousal immediately with winning on the poker machines.
    Learnt theory, in gambling, also plays into the concept of the "random ratio reinforcement schedule". This is where the use of unpredictability of wins and where the next level of excitement will come thereby formulating a persistence of playing on the poker machine (Haw, J. 2008)

Cognitive Modelling Theory

Cognitive modelling is a theory that there is a connection between the distortion of memories and cognitive capabilities and probability. (Gobet, 2011)

    It bases itself under the banner of misunderstanding experiences of random outcomes due to a distortion of reality.
    This results in false assumptions and relations between problem gambler's behaviour towards the treatment and control of their gambling addiction
    Cognitive Theory is based under four banners in the gambling sphere (APS Review Paper, 2010). These can be seen below.
    Illusion of Control: Addicts falsely inflate their abilities in influencing the overall outcome of games of probability
    Ritualistic behaviour to influence the outcome of the game: Cognitive superstition that ones behaviour before, during and after the game can influence the outcome of the machine.
    Selective recall of memories where favourable outcomes have occurred: This forced association forms the attention to be on winning the game and the skill involved in such.
    Expectations of winning after a series of losses.

Addiction Modelling

The addiction theory is self explanatory, in which that a gamblers exposure to gambling and winning can promote an urge to more. Through frequent participation, their overall control of the amount they gamble ceases and they have to undertake the activity to feel 'normal'. (Dickerson, 2006) Moreover, there is a effect that dopamine releases a 'high' for gamblers and it is this high that transfers into reinforcing the behaviour of a gambler. (Robinson, 2018) This high also plays into gamblers who even experience a loss at the machine. It promotes the idea that the gambler will 'chase the loss' ie- for every loss he experiences, he/she will make up the losses with an upcoming win. (Clark, 2007)

Reducing Gambling's Harm

Key Findings in this Section

Treatment is known to fall under three main goals, as explored in the Psychology of Gambling Report by the APS in 2010. They formed treatment to aim to:

    1) Prevent internal and external gambling-related issues for the individual
    2) Exercise a new promotion of newfound behaviours and policies related to gambling
    3) Protect the enabling of vulnerable people from gambling

Each patient is different and requires differing levels of interaction (Dickerson, 1993)

Currently there is no generalised treatment schedule for psychological treatment of gambling. Moreover, there is currently a single accepted school of thought that is involved in the intervention of gambling. This being total abstinence. This used to be viewed as the only viable and acceptable criteria to measure success on a gamblers wellbeing • It is the most common outcome when we address reducing the harm of gambling on individuals • For some problem gamblers, the retainment of the motivation to gamble for some is difficult because they simply enjoy gambling as a hobby (APS, 2010). This, thereby, introduces controlled gambling. This is where they manage their level of gambling to the amount of a usual casual gambler. Treatment, as of currently, has to also consider the impacts that gambling has in the face of pre-existing/coexisting illnesses the patient may have. (also known as comorbidities) (Petry, 2005) This is found impact current attempts to reduce gambler's harm to themselves and others within treatment. This complicated field has to consider the substantial hierarchy and value the effectiveness on treatment itself when multiple disorders are all co-dependent on each other. (Welte, 2001). By treating gamblers who suffer from multiple conditions, it is up to the practitioner to make a measured assessment on which condition to treat first to, overall, reduce the gamblers harm to themselves and others.

Taken directly from the interviews, it is clear that gamblers addiction is based heavily in the psychological front, with events such as "past trauma" (Gamblers Anonymous, 2019) being able to factor into starting the addiction. But in a general sense, there is this very individualistic nature to addiction. This could suggest a connection between emotion and addiction, which would link to this very personal perception that addicts have of themselves. In a general sense, no gambling addict is the same, and no addict has the same introduction to gambling. Each interview gave different definitions of what a problem gambler really is, which tends to beg the question "Is there truly a general definition for what makes a problem gambler?". Addicts have this personal perception of themselves that forced their addiction to occur, these are summarised into the following subheadings:

• Their personality created a gambling problem to be unique to them

• Their internal and external demons forced them to start gambling.

On a deeper level, each of these subheadings expand into more specific subtopics that analyse why these gamblers have this perception of themselves. Overarching subheading: Their internal and external demons forced them to start gambling

Subtopic One: They use gambling to escape their internal and external issues

"The most common phenomenon was these people all coming from a background with complex trauma, and all combability with mental health issues." - Law, 2019

Analysis and Key Findings

For many gamblers, gambling is used as an outlet from external issues. As we know, gamblers often have issues that "predate their addiction", and through the pre-known concept of this selective recall of their winnings of gambling, gamblers can simply escape their external issues. Through linking the research known about the cognitive model with these first hand experiences - this information concretes the idea that external and internal forces of a gambler psychologically trigger them. It triggers them to gamble to "alleviate anxiety and stress" - Hunt, 2019

This alleviation of issues forces addicts to go into the vicious cycle of problem gambling. The act of gambling is a paradox; for people the act of gambling reduces stress, however the act of losing money whilst gambling increases stress to try and chase the loss and make a win.

This can be seen as addicts are seeking this escape from their personality and external issues, they are innately led to gambling due to their negative sense of self. But, by gambling away money, it further solidifies their self-deprecating and depressive sense of self, which instinctively leads them back to gambling to escape their inner demons. In reality; problem gambling is a vicious cycle for many who are well into the addiction due to this desire for an escape.

This cognitive skew alters the gambler's perception of gambling and sends the gambler down a rabbit hole in which they think they can outsmart the machine. This concept of control, or more a lack of it, for problem gamblers actively gambling, can be seen through two main concepts; the overall context of where the poker machines are located enabling their cognitive skew of how much money can be won through gambling, and, problem gamblers perceived control over the machine.

• The true reason gamblers end up gambling is to cover up repressed emotions

• The treatment is a personal process of reinvention

• The treatment is a journey of discovery for individuals

This idea of reinvention plays true throughout these three subheadings. Affinity Diagram

Naturally, as a result of the sheer amount of data that was curated through the last research method, there is plentiful commonalities and patterns that have emerged. However, with no way to visually represent and understand this large amount of data from transcripts, mapping and literature, research analysis has to occur.

Affinity Diagramming is the obvious next step as a result from the reasons above. It allows "analysing data (breaking down of data into parts) and synthesis of the data (forming a coherent whole out of the parts) (Tomitsch, 2018). These two steps proved to be important as they would form the crux of the largest section of the report; the reframing stage.

The Methodology

Affinity diagramming works by putting raw data and insights on the lowest level, usually with a yellow post it note. After navigating through your data, you should be left with a massive wall of yellow post it notes which then can be grouped into clusters. These clusters' yellow post it notes show relationships and commonalities between the clusters. (Tomitsch, 2018)

Following this, you name each cluster with a theme and create the next level higher on the affinity diagram with these themes. These themes are usually written in another post it note colour. Due to the sheer amount of data I had, I then grouped these next level higher clusters into an even higher level theme. From this you can start seeing the common themes emerge in the data due to this grouping of commonalities. By analysing the data from these grouping of ideas and themes, you can synthesize and analyse the large amounts of data given by the research process.

Findings

The data synthesis and analysis process by affinity diagramming caused a massive finding to be made. By taking an overview look at the data itself (and the one word headings I've added for ease of reading and at the advice of my tutor), we can see there is a clear common pattern in the data.



Problem gambling naturally being a spectrum

The nature of the psychology of addiction is realistically a spectrum, with the causation of gambling. It depicts problem gamblers first being brought to gamble, through to their psychological experiences whilst gambling addictions, all the way to the final reinvention and treatment process. These three key aspects of what 'makes up' a problem gambler formulates this idea that the concept and psychology behind addiction is that it is a spectrum.

This idea of the spectrum of addiction is also intrinsically linked to the lack of definition behind what makes a problem gambler. Thereby, we can link these two concepts; problem gambling and addiction is on a spectrum; thereby that is why medical professions struggle to define clearly what dictates someone to have a problem with gambling. This finding was the biggest breakthrough in the research as I was beginning to realise the true nature of the psychology of addiction.

Because gambling addiction is a spectrum, there is no clear definition for a problem gambler

The concept of what psychologically enables problem gambling is only one tiny part in the larger sphere of gambling. By synthesizing and creating this "coherent whole" of the data, we can see that problem gambling is intrinsically personal, with every addict having their own experience and level of addiction. There is no direct commonalities between addicts, but our current treatment method counterintuitively generalises the treatment of problem gambling. This can be seen through the generalisation of even defining these gamblers. Interviewees called problem gamblers just to have an "addictive personality", had gone through some kind of "past trauma" and "serial gamblers", when in reality each have their own psychological history that had lead them to problem gambling.

This brings froth the idea of the spectrum, which is clearly seen in the affinity diagram and came as a direct result of the diagram. The spectrum is a start to end visualisation of gambling, the start being what made them begin gambling and the end being treatment out of addiction. Gamblers are positioned on in varying degrees due to their past experiences of gambling. This is because, again, addiction is this emotive construct. Emotion is inherently individualistic hence why there is a problem in defining these problem gamblers. If we cant even define what makes a problem gambler, how are we expected to even begin helping with guiding these gamblers into treatment?. This finding ties in both findings, both the spectrum analysis of gambling addiction, and the innate personal and emotive front of addiction.

Reframing

Archaeology of the problem

The situation illustrated at the beginning of this research was the enabling of problem gambling for addicts within the context of Australian pubs and clubs. As mentioned before by the divide of work, the psychology of enabling addicts were my part of the brief. The problem area can be seen simply with the net profit for these venues that they gain from gambling; with Chatswood RSL gaining 28 million dollars from gambling machines alone in 2017. Simply put, to forever keep gaining that margin of profit, the psychology of enabling these gamblers to keep feeding money into the pokies is a matter of life for these venues. Through the psychological enabling of learnt theories by these venues, such as the learnt behaviour model and selective cognitive recognition, these venues can gain a larger profit from gambling from these pathological gamblers. They do this through the lights, sounds and UI of these machines, where they position facilities around these machines and providing 'special treatment' for serial gamblers; such as free drinks, dinner and loyalty services. By exploiting these vulnerable individuals, the government makes large cuts on tax, local pubs stay open but, in all, the problem area is that these gamblers' addiction are very much enabled by these venues and forces them to be in this vicious cycle of gambling.

Current systems are in place to deter problem gamblers; these include the deterrent signs in and around the VIP room with slogans such as "Think of the odds" "Your chances are one in a million". Moreover, the self exclusion policy in many local pubs allow gamblers to electively ban themselves from the club. However, these solutions have a long way to go, in that, even with them implemented, problem gambling is still the worst in the world in Australia, with the numbers continuously on the rise.



By taking a psychological approach in order to understand and counter this problem of enabling of pathological gamblers, the approach for this study was to better understand what makes these gamblers tick, what defines problem gambling, and what treatment services are like. This gives a holistic view of the psychology of gambling and gamblers which, in hand, can allow us to better understand what enables these gamblers at these venues. From this, we can design a solution to create access and acknowledgement of treatment services.

Establishing the core paradox

• Because problem gambling can affect anyone - the treatment methods need to be generalised

• Because problem gambling has a host of personal emotional factors, victims need personalized treatment

This is intrinsically a paradox because due to the emotional value of pathological gambling, a factor that is inherently individualistic - we currently offer only generalised solutions and treatment methods for a concept that evidently very personal. By considering these paradoxes, we can later shape our reframe to consider the gambling and gambling treatment experience.

The context of the problem

The main stakeholders that have the largest say in this problem situation would be based in three main fields; the venues where gambling is enabled, the government from the tax revenue they gain from gamblers and individuals who offer treatment for pathological gambling. These groups in tandem have attempted to solve this problem of enabling gamblers, however as established before, they have had only a little effect on the gambling addiction community. These have worked to implement currently in place measures, such as the aforementioned signs around the VIP room and the influence of local hotels implementing thee self exclusion policies.

The Field

The field of problem gambling in Australia is large, with 200,000 individuals being affected by it every year. By expanding the context of the field to the wider society at large, we can radically begin to understand the true cultural and societal factors that can contribute to the stakeholder's enabling of problem gambling. Moreover, there are two other key players in the field, the government health funding for research about gambling, and, the treatment professionals. For gamblers, this concept of enabling in the venues is intrinsically based within the psychology of why they gamble. The initial kneejerk persona treatment professionals use to define problem gamblers are people with an "addictive personality" - however this generalises the treatment of gamblers as it begins to group all these addicts into one group. These currently in place systems to lump addicts into this field of understanding is done as a direct response by these treatment professionals as they have the largest dictation of how Australia has a whole treats gambling addiction. They also alter the communication with the other key stakeholder; the government health and research funding grants. By not truly understanding new research into what defines a problem gambler and, more from that, how our definition of problem gambling inherently enables them - treatment professionals and the government are in a vicious cycle of not realising the true nature of addiction.

This innately creates the following statement:

Because the generalisation of gambling addiction exists, treatment currently only views gambling addiction as a linear path in which all gamblers started gambling because of the same reasons.

In this example; it is clear that due to this enabling it opens up a larger and more pressing question. How can we begin to talk about what psychologically enables these gamblers when there is not any commonalities in the heuristics we use when we talk about the personality of these gamblers?

Themes

As mentioned in the field at play, there are main players that have say over the dictation of enabling problem gamblers within Australian Pubs. As discovered in the field, the new major player that has emerged is this lack of heuristics and definitions in defining what makes a problem gambler. This lack of definition promotes this lack of understanding in the rest of the field; such as what enables these gamblers. This concept cannot be looked past upon as the real reason why the other methods in removing the enabling of problem gambling in these venues is truly because of this lack of definition. Our generalised approach to treating problem gambling; weather that be those placards on the side of the machine, or the self exclusion policies - they all have not worked effectively because they are generalised.

We generalise addiction in a sense that we put it down to a personality trait (as seen with the idea that gamblers just have that "addictive mindset"). In reality, when we talk about addiction and psychology, there is several factors that can come into play into causing someone to fall in addiction. This is evident in the majority of problem gamblers because of the history of past psychological trauma many of them have experienced. More importantly, for many of them "symptoms of anxiety and depression predate their gambling" are a fact of life before gambling. This idea that addiction is just something that occurs is wrong. Addiction comes to fruition due to a multitude of phycological events in ones life.

In all, we can't begin to talk about fixing the enabling of gambling addiction when we truly do not understand the definition and cause of problem gambling to begin with.

Frames

As established in the theme from above, that being the struggle to define what problem gambling is, a frame was created to explore and curate ideas from.

• If problem gambling can effect anyone, we must offer specific and targeted treatment methods

• Because we are offering targeted and specific treatment methods, we can truly understand the personal emotional factors that made these gambler's start gambling in the first place.

Futures

If we continue to think along these lines, we need to be able to define what makes an addict first. One of the closest things to an adaptable service is Gambler's Anonymous service of "Screening" these problem gamblers when they first come into the clinic. By doing this, they know their exact background within their family history, in terms of psychology and addiction. This is an image of the first stepping stone that could be implemented in terms of personalizing treatment services. It could allow for early recognition of what made these individuals turn into problem gamblers.

By envisioning these personalised services, a clear definition of problem gambling could come from it. This could allow individuals to know what amount is considered to "be problematic". Moreover, by having easy access to these screening services, individuals with psychological triggers for addiction in their bloodline could formulate better understandings on how they are more likely to be turned into pathological gamblers themselves. This is due to many intrinsic patterns of the psychology relating to gambling can run in the genetic bloodline. This innately formulates a better outreach for at risk gamblers and to allow individuals in treatment to feel like their treatment is progressively helping them due to this personalisation effect.

Transformation

In reality, the clear long push that would be needed to redefine the entire sphere of addiction is possible, but in the short term from the basis of a university study; it isn't exactly feasible. However, what really would be the first stepping stone to revaluate the definition of problem gambling would be to get the members in the treatment agencies to adapt the newfound heuristics and models found in research. These include being able to judge the level of problem an individual has with gambling by using the theoretical models of illusion of control, impairing control and having an overall selective memory in recollection of wins.

These theories would be easy to implement to judge the level of addiction that is unique to each person because the psychologists and other clinicians already know they exist. In every meeting during the interview phase; every single member who had a hand in treating the psychology of addiction know that addicts exhibit behaviour that incorporate these models. The only difference was the way they use these models and behaviours to base their treatment off. Generalised treatment needs to be weeded out in place for the recognition of gambling being a very emotive concept that is personal for all. By using these models, it would put these problem gamblers on this aforementioned spectrum which begins the process of understanding addiction to be unique to each and every individual due to this emotional connection. between people and the way addiction shows itself. Thereby, by using this spectrum, individuals know how their treatment is truly going and how they are tracking. This also formulates a trigger for people to seek help as there is a qualitative way for them to see how their gambling may be considered a problem.

Integration

By integrating this concept of high personalisation in treatment processes and the ability to be able to know what level of problem gambler you are through a more definite and exact measurement of what really is a problem gambler, it upholds a better treatment for the patient and stakeholders. The wider society of the pub can still enjoy benefits from casual gamblers without enabling the community into addiction, the government can provide a more holistic and personal healthcare system that encompasses a better understanding of the true nature of addiction. Finally, the treatment agencies that give this treatment are truly going to be in the leading field of addiction understanding in the world. By keeping our definition and understanding of what addiction truly is, Australia can incorporate new research and better understanding into our treatment of addiction. Moreover, by truly understanding the nature of addiction, we can better engage with these venues to remove their innate enabling of problem gamblers in their venue.

Reflection

In conclusion, the enabling of pathological gamblers is not as simple as we once thought. Due to its nature as a complex and networked problem, there are several factors that need to be addressed in order to consider the enabling by these venues. Gambling, in a sense, bases itself off key emotional and psychological traits. What I learnt over the course of this research task was that a simple assumption based off the research question is not usually the best route. In the case of this psychological study, the research question almost became secondary to this overarching finding that "There is no clear definition for problem gambler". From this, it stems into other problem, "there are not common-place heuristics to treat problem gambling".

However, by taking a step back from these complex problems - the clear use of different 'rounds' of research methodologies, such as adapting interview questions and forever literature researching, it allowed me to form a cohesive argument in the uncovering of the true nature behind the problem of gambling in Australia. This gives way to understanding the larger sphere of gambling. Naturally, with more time this research could have delved even deeper into why there is this lack of definition behind gambling, but due to the uncovering of this insight only weeks ago by myself - there is only so much time to continuous research. However, the sheer fact of uncovering this deeper level insight can install newfound opportunities in the creation of the interface and other products for the upcoming project.
